                    Base YANG Model for any Service
                   draft-lambrechts-onsen-svc-yang-00

Abstract

   The L2SM and L3SM YANG models provide a standardized approach to
   modeling L2VPN and L3VPN services.  However, these models do not
   interact with each other, and they are not designed to be extended to
   model other types of services.  This document proposes a base YANG
   model for any service, which can be used as a starting point for
   modeling specific customer services.

1.  Introduction

   This document defines a modular service modeling stack that extracts
   technology-agnostic constructs into a base ietf-svc module, separates
   Ethernet-specific treatment into ietf-eth-svc, and layers VPN-
   oriented intent in ietf-vpn-svc, with bis versions of L2VPN and L3VPN
   services augmenting the shared foundation.  The goals are to reduce
   duplication between existing L2SM and L3SM work, enable new service
   types to reuse common abstractions, and let operators expose a
   consistent customer-facing interface while retaining flexibility for
   technology-specific extensions.

2.  Limitations of the current Service Models

2.1.  Limited Interoperability and Extensibility

   The YANG Service Models in [RFC8299] (L3SM) and [RFC8466] (L2SM)
   capture Layer 3 and Layer 2 VPN service intent at an abstraction
   level that is directly applicable to the agreements between an
   operator and their customers.  These models individually cover all
   the inputs to deliver either L2VPN or L3VPN services.  However, a
   provider who wants to offer both types of services must manage two
   separate models and/or request their customers to enter a lot of
   duplicate information.  It is difficult to express certain

   relationships between the L2VPN and L3VPN services being ordered,
   such as diversity or bandwidth constraints.  More broadly, operators
   and their customers would benefit from a common service modeling
   framework that can be reused across different service types, and that
   can capture the relationships and constraints.  Examples services
   that could be modeled on top of the same base include the already
   standardized L2VPN and L3VPN services, but also non-VPN services such
   as (business) internet access, cloud connectivity, or even optical
   wavelength services.  The common service model would also allow
   operators to maintain a consistent customer-facing interface across
   different service types, and to more easily introduce new service
   types in the future.

2.2.  Missing support for network layering

   The current L2SM and L3SM models do not have a way to express the
   relationships between different service layers.  For example, an
   operator may want to specify that an L3VPN service should be
   delivered over a specific L2VPN service, or that an SD-WAN service
   should be delivered over a specific internet access service or MPLS-
   based L3VPN.  This lack of support for network layering makes it
   difficult to model and manage complex service offerings that involve
   multiple layers of abstraction and different technologies.

2.3.  Over-abstraction hinders clear agreement

   The L3SM abstracts away from the underlying network technologies and
   focuses on the service intent.  While this is a strength in terms of
   technology-agnostic modeling, it can also make it difficult to
   capture certain service attributes that are relevant to the customer
   or to the delivery of the service.

   For example, the L3SM has no method of specifying a port and/or VLAN
   on a Site Device on which a Site Network Access should be delivered.
   This can lead to ambiguity in the service specification, and it can
   make it difficult for operators to ensure that the service is
   delivered according to the customer's intent.

   The constraints mechanism goes through great lengths to avoid
   identifying the specific (number of) bearers that should be used to
   deliver a service.  The ordering process for many operators today is
   much more explicit in naming the bearers, along with an explicit
   mapping of each Site Network Access to those bearers.  At least
   giving the purchaser the option of providing their requirements in
   such an explicit manner would guarantee that the operator can deliver
   a service according to the customer's actual requirements.

3.  Proposed solution

   This document revisits the L3SM and L2SM models and introduces a
   family of YANG modules that factor out the common service concepts
   into a shared base.  The new ietf-svc module provides a technology-
   agnostic service skeleton covering sites, site-network- accesses,
   diversity constraints, and bearer hints.  Ethernet-centric constructs
   such as bandwidth profiles and QoS classification are moved into a
   dedicated ietf-eth-svc module so that Ethernet- based services can
   share the same treatment primitives without cluttering the core
   service skeleton.

   The ietf-vpn-svc module builds on that base to capture VPN-oriented
   attributes such as service type, topology, cloud or extranet
   reachability, carriers' carrier, and policy filters.  Bis versions of
   ietf-l3vpn-svc and ietf-l2vpn-svc then augment the shared service
   framework with technology-specific parameters.  The L3VPN module
   retains the routing, multicast, address-allocation, NAT, and BFD
   constructs from [RFC8299].  The L2VPN module retains the Ethernet-
   and OAM-specific behaviors from [RFC8466] (e.g., bundling, CE-VLAN
   preservation, L2CP handling, CFM, and Y.1731) while leveraging the
   shared service skeleton, the Ethernet treatment constructs in ietf-
   eth-svc, and the VPN service identities from ietf-vpn-svc.

   The augment relationships across the modules are shown in Figure 1;
   the path is linear, with ietf-svc at the root, ietf-eth-svc
   augmenting the base skeleton, and ietf-vpn-svc augmenting both ietf-
   eth-svc and ietf-svc before the VPN-specific bis modules attach.

   module: ietf-svc                      (Base service skeleton)
     +-- augment: ietf-eth-svc           (Ethernet attributes, e.g QoS)
       +-- augment: ietf-vpn-svc         (VPN service common attributes)
         +-- augment: ietf-l2vpn-svc     (L2VPN-specific attributes)
         +-- augment: ietf-l3vpn-svc     (L3VPN-specific attributes)

            Figure 1: Service module augmentation relationships

   By aligning the L2SM and L3SM models on a common foundation, this
   work reduces duplication, improves consistency of service exposure to
   customers and OSS/BSS systems, and simplifies the introduction of
   future service types that can reuse the same base abstractions.

4.  Relationships to other work

   The network layer models, iet-l3vpn-ntw [RFC9182] and iet-l2vpn-ntw
   [RFC9291] MAY benefit from a similar modularization approach, but
   they are outside the scope of this document.

6.  Security Considerations

   The security posture of this document follows the guidance in the
   L3SM [RFC8299].  The NETCONF Access Control Model (NACM) [RFC6536]
   SHOULD be used to restrict protocol operations and data nodes to
   authorized principals.

   New augments added to the base service skeleton MUST describe any
   additional security-sensitive leaves and specify suitable NACM rules.
   When encryption or authentication material is conveyed (e.g., keys or
   credentials), operators MUST ensure secure transport and operational
   processes (rotation, revocation, audit) consistent with their policy.
